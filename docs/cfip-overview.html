What is CFIP?

Every day, providers order medications that get denied by insurance payers — sometimes weeks after the order was placed. The provider must then appeal, gather documentation, or switch the patient to a different drug. This delay harms patients and wastes clinical time.

CFIP intercepts this problem at the point of prescribing. The moment a provider selects a medication in Epic, CFIP fires in the background, checks the patient's clinical data against the payer's prior authorization criteria, and returns an actionable card — before the order is even submitted.

Without CFIP

Provider orders drug → submits PA → payer denies 2 weeks later → appeal → patient delay → rework.

With CFIP

Provider selects drug → CFIP card appears instantly → provider fixes gaps before submitting → PA approved first time.

Three Intelligence Pipelines (Phase 4)

GLP-1 Prior Authorization — For drugs like Ozempic and Wegovy, CFIP checks whether the patient meets the payer's PA criteria: metformin step therapy, A1C threshold, BMI, and coverage. Returns a denial risk score and PA bundle status.
PGx Safety (Pharmacogenomics) — For drugs like clopidogrel (Plavix), CFIP checks the patient's genome against CPIC guidelines. If the patient can't metabolize the drug due to a gene variant, CFIP fires a critical safety alert and recommends alternatives.
Generic Info Card — For all other drugs, CFIP returns a lightweight info card noting that specific intelligence isn't yet available. Phases 5+ expand coverage.

End-to-End Workflow

Here is exactly what happens from the moment a provider opens the prescribing screen to when they see the CFIP card.

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1

Provider opens order entry in Epic

A cardiologist searches for "clopidogrel" or a GP types "Ozempic". The moment the drug is selected (before submitting), Epic detects an order-select event.

2

Epic fires a CDS Hook at CFIP

Epic sends a POST /cds-services/cfip-order-intelligence with a JSON payload containing: the patient ID, the draft medication order, and pre-fetched FHIR data (patient demographics, active medications).

3

CFIP classifies the drug

CFIP extracts the drug name and RxNorm code from the order. The Drug Classifier maps it to a class: glp1, pgx_sensitive, oncology, or standard. This determines which pipeline runs next.

4

CFIP fetches the patient's clinical data from Epic FHIR

CFIP calls Epic's FHIR R4 API: GET /Patient/{id}, GET /Observation?code=4548-4 (A1C), GET /Observation?code=39156-5 (BMI), GET /MedicationRequest (medication history), GET /Coverage (insurance). If data is missing, a synthetic overlay fills gaps for the demo.

5

The correct pipeline runs

GLP-1: Weighted denial scorer checks 5 PA criteria against payer rules stored in SQLite. Specialty PA Builder assembles a structured PA bundle.
PGx: CPIC engine checks the patient's diplotype against the pharmacogenomics knowledge base.

6

CFIP returns a CDS Card to Epic

The card appears inside Epic's order entry screen — no new window, no separate system. The provider sees the denial risk score, PA status, or safety alert without leaving their workflow. The card has action buttons: "Submit PA Now", "Review Documentation", or "Switch to Prasugrel".

Scenario A — GLP-1 Prior Authorization (Ozempic)

A
Drug: Ozempic (semaglutide 0.5mg injection)  |  Pipeline: GLP-1 PA  |  Payer: UnitedHealthcare

Clinical Context

Patient Derrick Lin is being prescribed Ozempic for Type 2 Diabetes. Ozempic costs ~$900/month list price — UnitedHealthcare requires Prior Authorization before covering it.

What CFIP Checks (UHC GLP-1 PA Criteria)

CriterionRequiredFound in Patient RecordResult
Step therapy — metformin trial ≥ 90 days 180 days on metformin ✅ Met
A1C blood test ≥ 7.0% within 6 months 5.1% — over 6 years old ❌ Not Met
No prior denials Clean history 0 prior denials ✅ Met
Insurance coverage Active, drug on formulary UHC active, formulary confirmed ✅ Met
💡
Why A1C matters: A1C (Glycated Hemoglobin) is a blood test showing average blood sugar over 3 months. Payers require A1C ≥ 7.0% as proof the patient has uncontrolled Type 2 Diabetes — the approved indication for Ozempic. This patient's A1C is 5.1% (normal range), meaning the payer has no clinical evidence of diabetes. The result is also 6+ years old, failing the recency requirement. A real payer would deny this order.

Scenario B — PGx Safety Alert (Clopidogrel)

B
Drug: Clopidogrel (Plavix) 75mg  |  Pipeline: Pharmacogenomics  |  Standard: CPIC Guidelines

Clinical Context

A cardiologist is prescribing clopidogrel (Plavix) after a patient has a cardiac stent placed. Clopidogrel is a standard antiplatelet drug — without it, blood clots can form on the stent. But clopidogrel is a prodrug: it must be activated by the CYP2C19 liver enzyme before it works.

What CPIC Says (Evidence Level 1A)

GeneDiplotypeMetabolizer StatusCPIC Recommendation
CYP2C19 *1/*1 Normal metabolizer Use clopidogrel at standard dose
CYP2C19 *1/*2 Intermediate metabolizer Consider alternative antiplatelet agent
CYP2C19 *2/*2 Poor metabolizer AVOID — use prasugrel or ticagrelor
CYP2C19 *1/*17 Rapid metabolizer Use clopidogrel at standard dose
🧬
What is *2/*2? The CYP2C19 gene comes in variants called alleles. *1 is the normal functioning copy. *2 is a non-functional variant. This patient inherited two *2 copies — one from each parent — meaning they have essentially zero CYP2C19 enzyme activity. ~2-3% of the population are poor metabolizers. Without PGx testing, there's no way to know without checking the genome.

Actual CDS Card Returned to Epic

critical
⚠️ Clopidogrel: CYP2C19 Poor Metabolizer — Drug Ineffective
⚠️ PGx Safety Alert — Clopidogrel Gene: CYP2C19 | Status: Poor Metabolizer | Diplotype: *2/*2 Clinical impact: This patient is a CYP2C19 poor metabolizer. Clopidogrel requires hepatic activation by CYP2C19 — poor metabolizers cannot convert clopidogrel to its active form. Standard doses provide <10% of normal platelet inhibition, significantly increasing risk of stent thrombosis. Recommended alternatives: • Prasugrel (does not require CYP2C19 activation) • Ticagrelor (direct-acting, no metabolism required) CPIC Evidence Level: 1A (cpicpgx.org)

Why This Matters — Patient Safety

Without CFIP: Provider prescribes clopidogrel. Patient takes it for months believing they are protected against stent thrombosis. The drug provides <10% of expected platelet inhibition. A blood clot forms on the stent (stent thrombosis) — a life-threatening cardiac emergency. No one knew the patient couldn't metabolize the drug.
With CFIP: The critical card appears at the moment of prescribing. The cardiologist switches to prasugrel or ticagrelor — both equally effective without requiring CYP2C19 activation. Patient is protected. The PGx result is stored and reused for all future prescribing decisions.

Why We Move to Agentic in Phase 5

Phases 3 and 4 used hand-coded pipelines — deterministic rule sets for a specific drug class and payer combination. This works well for a demo but hits a wall in production.

The Payer × Drug Matrix Problem

In the real world, PA criteria vary by payer and by drug class. Every cell in this matrix is a different rule set:

Drug Class UnitedHealthcare Aetna Blue Cross Cigna Humana
GLP-1 agonists ✓ A1C ≥7.0% ✓ A1C ≥8.0% ✓ BMI ≥35 ✓ 2 drug failures ✓ varies
Biologics (TNF) ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓
Oncology ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓
Opioids ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓
⚠️
Each cell is a separate coded rule set. Payers update their criteria quarterly. A production system covering 5 payers × 20 drug classes = 100 rule sets to maintain. That is a full-time team just keeping up with policy updates — before writing a single line of clinical logic.

What the Agentic Orchestrator Changes

Phase 5 introduces an LLM-powered orchestrator (Claude / GPT-4) that replaces the hand-coded routing logic. Instead of if payer == "Aetna" and drug_class == "glp1", the agent:

Rule-Based Limits

  • 100+ rule files to maintain
  • Payer updates require code changes
  • New drug = new sprint
  • Can't reason over free-text policies
  • No appeal letter generation

Agentic Capabilities

  • Reads payer policy PDFs directly
  • New payer = upload document
  • New drug = no code change
  • Generates PA appeal letters
  • Explains its own reasoning
🎯
The goal of Phase 5: Adding a new drug/payer combination should be a configuration problem (upload a policy document), not a development problem (write and deploy new code). The rule-based phases (3 and 4) prove the concept and establish the data contracts. The agentic phase makes it commercially scalable.

Phase Roadmap

2

SQLite payer rules. Weighted denial scorer (5 factors, 100-point model). Synthetic data overlay. Real FHIR data fetch. PA bundle assembly. Denial card composer. 47 tests.

4

CPIC pharmacogenomics engine. CYP2C19/clopidogrel safety checking. Drug classifier routing (glp1 / pgx_sensitive / oncology). Specialty PA builder. Two-scenario test harness with Run All button.

5

Phase 5 — Agentic Orchestrator (Next)

LLM-powered agent replaces hand-coded routing. Tools: FHIR fetcher, PA submitter, appeal letter generator. Works for any drug × payer combination. OpenAI / Claude API integration.

Full SMART on FHIR launch context. Provider-facing React app embedded in Epic sidebar. Shows full analysis, PA submission history, appeal status, cost transparency.
